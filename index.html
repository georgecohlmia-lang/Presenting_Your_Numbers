01

The Spouse or Partner Conversation

Hardest first · emotional weight

The mistake is almost never too much honesty.

Start here, because this conversation carries the most emotional weight and is where founders make the most mistakes. The mistake is almost always numbers that are too optimistic because you're afraid of the reaction, or too vague because you haven't done the work. Both leave them without the information they need to share this decision with you.

01BEAT ONE · HERE'S THE PLAN

Present it the same way you'd present to a sophisticated advisor. Not dumbed down, not inflated.

Open with this:

I want to walk you through where we actually are financially. Not the version I'd give someone at a dinner party — the same version I'd show a serious advisor. I need you to see the real numbers, because you carry this with me.

Then walk through, in order:

Our runway right now is . Our total monthly burn is — that's for us personally and for the business. My base case has revenue reaching by month . That's the number where our exposure meaningfully changes.

Use the actual numbers from your Runway Playbook. If you haven't done that work yet, do it before this conversation — not during.

02BEAT TWO · HERE'S WHAT COULD GO WRONG

Walk them through the Oh-No scenario explicitly. This is the most important signal of credibility you can send.

Transition gently:

Now let me show you what I'd do if things go sideways — because they might, and I've thought about it. This isn't me being pessimistic. It's me showing you I have a plan, not just optimism.

Then be concrete:

If sales come in 40 to 50% slower than my base case for four or five months in a row — which is the pattern that catches founders off guard — the first thing I cut is . The second is . Before the money runs out, I'm having with .

Pull these directly from your Oh-No Response Plan. Having them memorized, not read, signals you've actually thought about this.

03BEAT THREE · HERE'S WHAT I NEED FROM YOU

Not money — support. Name it explicitly, or it doesn't happen.

Ask directly:

When month six is hard — and some months will be — here's what I'd ask from you. A good partner in this moment looks like
Need a starting point?
And what I'd ask you not to do:
Need a starting point?

These conversations almost never happen explicitly. Founders pay for that later with misunderstanding and decisions made in emotional fog. Name what you need now.

02

The Co-Founder Conversation

Shared reality · stress-test together

You're not presenting numbers. You're establishing a shared reality.

Different in tone, similar in structure. The goal isn't agreement — it's alignment. Your assumptions reveal your priorities. Where you've been optimistic, where you've been conservative, what you've planned for — all of it communicates how you think about the business.

01BEAT ONE · HERE'S THE PLAN

Don't present it — walk through it together. The goal is alignment, not agreement.

Open by reframing the meeting:

I want to walk through the model with you, not present it to you. I need you to push on my assumptions — not to validate them. Where I've been optimistic, tell me. Where I've been conservative, tell me that too. We'll both run this business better if we've stress-tested this together than if one of us hands over a polished version and the other nods.

Then flag the two or three assumptions you're least sure about:

The assumptions I'm least certain about are:
Need a starting point?

Naming uncertainty first signals that this is a real conversation, not a sales pitch. A good co-founder relaxes when you do this.

02BEAT TWO · HERE'S WHERE I'D WANT YOU TO PUSH

Ask explicitly which assumptions they'd change, and why.

Make it a real request:

If you had to change three assumptions in this model, which three would you change? Don't soften it. If the base case feels too optimistic to you, I need to hear that now — not in month eight when we're both looking at a miss.

And a second question that often surfaces more:

What would you have planned for that I haven't?

Two people who've stress-tested a model together execute from it with commitment. One person who received a polished presentation and nodded along is a problem waiting to emerge.

03BEAT THREE · WHAT WE AGREE ON GOING FORWARD

Pre-decide the decision cadence. Who looks at what, when.

Close with process:

Here's what I'd propose for how we operate from these numbers. We review pipeline and runway . If we're trending toward Oh-No for two consecutive review periods, we convene and run the response plan together. If we hit the Best Case leading indicators, we have an investment conversation before we commit to spending anything. Does that cadence work for you, or would you build it differently?

Agreeing on cadence now prevents the classic failure mode: one founder sees the problem three months before the other one is willing to look at it.

03

The Early Backer or Advisor Conversation

Rigor over polish · honesty over certainty

A sophisticated backer is not impressed by polish. They're impressed by fluency.

The instinct is to project confidence and shine the model up. Push back on that instinct. A sophisticated backer has seen hundreds of year-one projections — what impresses them is a founder who speaks fluently about their assumptions and has thought through what happens when reality diverges.

01BEAT ONE · HERE'S THE PLAN AND HERE'S MY METHODOLOGY

Know your model cold. Lead with how you built it, not what it projects.

Open with methodology, not results:

Before I walk you through the numbers, I want to tell you how I built them — because the methodology matters more than the projection. I started from my actual pipeline, not a market size calculation. My close rates are weighted by stage. My sales cycle assumption is based on . Where I had to estimate, I'll flag it.

Then the headline figures:

Base case: I reach by month . Runway at current burn is . Now let me show you where the model could be wrong.

A backer hears "let me show you where the model could be wrong" and their shoulders drop half an inch. You've just signaled you're not selling them — you're thinking with them.

02BEAT TWO · WALK THEM THROUGH THE OH-NO BEFORE THEY ASK

Bring up the conservative scenario first. It shows you've looked at it squarely.

Get there before they do:

My Oh-No scenario is sales running 40–50% slower than base for four to five consecutive months. In that case, my runway compresses to . Here's what I'd do first: . Here's my trigger point for a funding conversation: — not when the money is gone.

Investors don't expect certainty. They expect rigor. A specific trigger point is worth more than a polished forecast.

03BEAT THREE · HERE'S WHERE I NEED YOUR HELP

Be specific. Backers want to feel useful, not pitched.

Ask for exactly what you need:

What I'd most value from you right now is
Need a starting point?

A specific ask gets a specific yes or no. A vague ask gets polite deflection. Your Tuesday self will thank your today self.

04

Questions You Don't Want to Get

Three patterns · practiced responses

Every one of these conversations will produce at least one question you don't want to answer. Three categories come up most. The response pattern matters more than the specific words.

01 "How confident are you in that number?" (when you're not)

Don't defend it more aggressively than you believe it. Say so directly. Intellectual honesty builds more trust than false certainty — every time.

I'm less certain about this number than I'd like to be. Here's why I chose it as a starting point: [your reasoning]. Here's what would cause me to revise it: [the specific data point you're watching].

The minute you name the uncertainty, the room relaxes. The person stops trying to catch you being wrong, and starts trying to help you be right.

02 "What if [downside scenario you haven't modeled]?"

Answer with your framework, not a number you manufacture under pressure. The framework is more reassuring than a fabricated answer.

I haven't modeled that exact scenario, but here's how I'd think through it. First, I'd look at [the variable most affected]. My trigger point would be [specific indicator], and my first action would be [specific cost cut or revenue lever]. I can build the full model if it'd help us think more precisely.

Offering to build the model is better than pretending you already have. The offer signals you take the question seriously.

03 "That timeline feels long." (timeline pressure)

Address the emotion before the math. Leading with the defense makes people feel dismissed. Leading with acknowledgment makes them feel heard.

I hear you — it does feel long, and I've sat with that myself. Let me tell you why I chose these assumptions anyway: [the actual reasoning]. If you see where I'm being too conservative, I genuinely want to hear it.

"I hear you" is not a capitulation. It's the move that lets you be honest about the math without the other person feeling unheard.

05

What Not to Do

Three things that damage trust for months

Don't show only the base case

The moment someone discovers there were numbers you chose not to show, the trust dynamic shifts permanently. Show all three scenarios every time.

Don't promise certainty you don't have

"This will work by month nine" is a commitment you can't make. "My base case gets us there and here's what I'm watching" is honest. Only one holds up when month nine arrives differently than planned.

Don't treat it as a one-time conversation

Your numbers change as reality comes in. Monthly updates, honest check-ins on which scenario you're actually tracking toward. Treat it as an ongoing practice, not a one-time presentation.
